Section 01

Executive summary

Heliode sells AI computing power and owns the process behind delivering it. AI inference — running trained models in production — is a steady, 24/7, latency-sensitive load that is growing faster than the industry can build for. The binding constraint isn't chips alone; it's power and time-to-build. New grid interconnections take years; new data centers take longer.

We attack that gap in two de-risked phases. Phase 1 (now): an asset-light managed brokerage — source GPU capacity on the open market, wrap it in a managed service, and resell it to inference teams below their current cloud bill. This earns revenue immediately and, critically, proves and secures the demand. Phase 2 (next): deploy owned GPU nodes on power-ready sites — leased commercial space first, and service-upgraded, self-generating residential sites as an expansion channel — financed against contracted compute revenue.

Section 02

The opportunity — a quantified bottleneck

Demand for AI compute is exploding, and the largest research houses have put numbers to the supply constraint.

  • Global data-center capex could reach ~$7 trillion by 2030; capacity demand nearly triples from ~82 GW (2025) to ~219 GW (2030), with roughly 70% of new demand driven by AI.1
  • In a survey of 120 U.S. power-company and data-center executives, grid stress was the leading challenge to development — the binding constraint is power and interconnection, not capital.2
  • Inference is projected to become ~100× the workload volume of training — steady, latency-sensitive, 24/7 demand — while reported GPU utilization often runs under 50%, so capacity is both scarce and poorly used.3,4
  • The architecture is shifting toward latency-optimized "inference zones" near population centers, not only remote mega-campuses.3
The opening: centralized supply is gated on multi-year power and permitting. Placing compute where heavy power already exists — and managing the messy deployment most incumbents avoid — sidesteps the queue and serves the underserved long tail of seed-to-Series-A AI teams.

Section 03

Vision & operating philosophy

We reason from first principles: the question is not "how do we build a data center?" but "what actually limits AI compute, and what's the fastest way around it?" The answer is power and time. So we go where power already exists and stand compute up in days.

The precedent: distributed assets, coordinated by software

This model is proven. Tesla's Virtual Power Plant aggregates tens of thousands of distributed home batteries and solar into a single, AI-coordinated grid asset; in July 2025, California VPPs delivered >535 MW, with Powerwall homes alone providing ~500 MW.5 Grid-scale capacity, assembled entirely from distributed hardware. Heliode applies the identical playbook to compute: a coordinated fleet of nodes behaving like one cloud.

It is also already being funded. In 2026, Span and NVIDIA launched XFRA — a distributed data-center network running GPUs on existing electrical capacity in homes and small commercial space, targeting ~1 GW by 2027.6 That a chip-maker of NVIDIA's scale is capitalizing the "go where the power is" thesis validates the market. Heliode's wedge is a different layer: the managed, demand-side service that signs and serves the end inference teams — and a site model that pairs robust upgraded service with on-site self-generation.

Section 04

Product & service

We sell access to compute, measured in GPU-hours, wrapped in a managed service. The customer ships their model; we handle the metal. "Managed" means provisioning, orchestration, monitoring and reliability, one endpoint, and one bill — the layer the cheap marketplaces lack and the hyperscalers overcharge for.

The arbitrage

The same GPU-hour costs wildly different amounts by source. An H100-hour runs roughly ~$2 wholesale on marketplaces; we resell it managed at ~$3, still well under the $5–10 a hyperscaler charges. The buyer saves; we keep ~$1/GPU-hr plus the managed-service premium.

We match the GPU to the workload: power-efficient L40S nodes for the broad inference market (≈⅓ the power and lower capex), and H100 / H200 for the most demanding models — more revenue-generating compute per kilowatt on power-constrained sites.

Section 05

The model & site strategy

Two phases

  1. Phase 1 — Managed brokerage (now). Asset-light resale of managed capacity. Immediate revenue, near-zero CAC under a quote-first offer, and the demand proof that finances Phase 2.
  2. Phase 2 — Owned regional capacity (next). Owned GPU nodes on power-ready sites, financed against signed offtake. We own the hardware and the multi-year customer relationship; hardware refreshes every ~5 years.

Site archetypes

A node is an 8-GPU server (~10 kW, ~$250K). The site only needs power — and we bring our own install crew so electrical work is never a landlord dependency.

  • Archetype A — leased commercial / light-industrial (now): buildings already carrying heavy 3-phase service. Lease, install, go live in days; fewer noise/zoning frictions; fastest path to first owned nodes.
  • Archetype B — service-upgraded residential, self-generating (expansion): our crew upgrades a home to 400A service (making it a commercial-grade power customer), deploys the node, and pairs it with max-fit rooftop solar + battery via a solar partner (e.g. Sunrun). The homeowner gets solar and backup; we get a power-ready site and a distribution channel competitors can't easily assemble.
Honesty note — solar
A node runs 24/7 (~240 kWh/day); ~20 kW of rooftop solar yields on the order of 100 kWh/day. On-site solar + battery is a meaningful energy-cost offset and resilience layer, not a full off-grid supply. The upgraded utility service remains the primary feed; we underwrite to grid power and treat self-generation as upside, with the partner carrying solar/storage capital and install.
Section 06

Technical feasibility

The orchestration-and-billing software that makes many nodes behave like one cloud sits on a real, published research base — this is engineering, not speculation.

  • EdgeFlow — distributed inference for DAG-structured DNNs across heterogeneous edge devices (IEEE INFOCOM 2022).7
  • Federated Learning in Edge Computing — a peer-reviewed systematic survey of distributed ML across cloud, edge, and end devices.8
  • MDI-LLM — model-distributed inference deploying LLMs across low-power distributed nodes via pipeline parallelism (arXiv).9
  • Production frameworks (NVIDIA FLARE, FEDML/TensorOpera) show the orchestration tooling exists today.

This de-risks the mandate of the first critical hire — an infrastructure / compute lead who owns that software layer.

Section 07

Unit economics

Phase 1 — brokerage

Per GPU-hour: pay ~$2, sell ~$3 → ~$1 gross margin. One H100 run continuously (~730 hrs/mo) ≈ ~$730 gross margin per GPU per month. A first real month of 5–6 clients at ~8 GPUs each (~48 GPUs) ≈ ~$105K revenue, ~$70K cost, ~$35K gross profit — at near-zero CAC, since paid capacity only spins up once a customer says yes.

Section 09

Go-to-market

We sell across two demand tiers:

  • Inference teams (now): AI startups, in-house ML teams, and studios already spending on GPUs and feeling it. The motion is prospect → open → discover → pitch → quote → close, anchored on the buyer's current bill. The quote-first offer means paid capacity only spins up once a customer commits.
  • Hyperscalers & neoclouds (scale offtake): the cloud giants are power-starved and increasingly buy distributed/third-party capacity to close their own speed-to-power gap (the same buyers XFRA targets). A single hyperscaler or neocloud contract can anchor whole sites or regions — exactly the contracted offtake that makes owned capacity financeable. We pursue these as anchor tenants for the network while the inference-team book builds breadth and margin.

Launch metros: Austin (base camp), then SF Bay Area, Seattle, Boston, and Dallas — dense clusters of seed-to-Series-A AI companies. Distribution is run through a commission-based founding sales team earning recurring overrides and earned equity, tracked in an internal Kanban pipeline.

Section 10

Competition

We compete with hyperscalers (price), neoclouds (price + locality), DIY/spot (we remove the headache), and emerging distributed players such as XFRA (different layer). Our edge is the combination — cheaper + managed + local + fast to deploy — plus ownership of distribution.

On XFRA specifically: Span + NVIDIA are building distributed supply (selling capacity to clouds, residential-led). Heliode operates the demand and managed layer (signing and serving end inference teams) and can source from multiple supplies — potentially including networks like theirs. Their capital validates the thesis; our moat is the customer relationship and multi-source deployment they don't own.

Section 13

Risks & dependencies

  • Offtake first. A node is a ~$250K brick without a contracted buyer. The brokerage exists to prove and secure demand before deployment.
  • Site/host acquisition. The hardest problem; mitigated by starting on power-ready commercial sites and bringing our own install crew.
  • Hardware cost. ~$250K is a planning number — ground-truth with vendor quotes before committing capital.
  • Financing. Hardware is financed against contracts; sequence the build behind signed demand.
  • Price decline / GPU supply. Plan for ~6%/yr decline; solve sourcing and allocation early without a single point of failure.
  • Distribution is the moat, not price. Compute commoditizes; the customer and site network is the durable asset.
