The problem

Compute is the bottleneck — and power, not chips, is the constraint.

Inference is overtaking training as the dominant AI workload: steady, 24/7, latency-sensitive, exploding. Meanwhile centralized supply can't keep up — grid interconnection runs 5–7 years and new data centers are multi-year, multi-billion-dollar builds.

The opening

The power is already at the curb.

Distributed compute sidesteps the bottleneck: deploy GPU nodes on power-ready sites, next to the teams that need them, in days. Big players (NVIDIA, others) are now testing distributed/home compute — the thesis is validating.

The model — two phases
Now · Phase 1

Managed brokerage

Source GPU capacity on the open market, run it as a managed service, and resell to inference teams with a rate-beat guarantee. Asset-light, revenue now — and it proves demand and signs the offtake that de-risks Phase 2.

Next · Phase 2

Owned distributed network

Deploy owned, battery-first GPU nodes on power-ready sites (commercial first, homes later). We own the hardware and the customer relationship; hardware is financed against contracted compute, the way solar is financed against a PPA.

Why it works

The moat is distribution.

A sales-and-install engine that puts compute where it's needed — and owns the host network and customer relationships. Compute will commoditize; being the one who can deploy it, fast, won't. Vivint's recurring-revenue engine × SolarCity's distributed-asset financing, pointed at AI's biggest constraint.

Economics & upside

Strong unit economics, financed against contracts.

Base case per node: ~$9.8K/mo gross, ~$7.6K/mo net, payback ~year 3, ~+$170K net over 5 years. Scaling to 1,000+ nodes across Austin, Dallas, Boston and California drives the network into nine figures of annual net. (Illustrative; requires contracted offtake.)

Team & what's next
