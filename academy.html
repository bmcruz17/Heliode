Module 01

The big idea — reasoning from first principles

The one sentence: Heliode sells AI computing power, and is building the distributed network that produces it — close to where the demand already is. That computing power comes from GPUs — graphics processing units, the specialized chips that do the heavy parallel math behind modern AI. Everything we do is about getting more of them, running them, and getting that power to the companies that need it.

What "first principles" means here

Instead of copying how others build compute (giant centralized data centers), we strip the problem to physics and economics and rebuild from there. The question isn't "how do we build a data center?" It's "what actually limits AI compute, and what's the fastest way around it?"

The real bottleneck

Modern AI runs on GPUs, and demand is exploding. But you can't conjure more capacity on demand: a GPU needs a building, and a building needs power — and power is the slow part. New grid hookups (interconnection) can take 5–7 years; a new data center is a multi-year, multi-billion-dollar build. So the binding constraint on AI isn't only chips — it's power and time-to-build.

The move

If power and build-time are the constraint, don't fight the grid — go where power already exists, or bring it fast. Light-commercial buildings already carry heavy electrical service (high-amperage, three-phase). On residential sites, our own crew upgrades the home to 400A commercial-grade service and pairs the node with rooftop solar + battery. Either way we put GPU capacity next to the teams that need it and stand up compute in days, not years. Proximity also helps inference, which is latency-sensitive — closer compute can mean faster responses.

Where the moat is

The chips aren't the moat — anyone can buy those. The moat is distribution: sourcing sites, signing them, installing, and operating many locations as if they were one data center. That's the unglamorous work incumbents avoid, which is exactly why it's defensible. We own the site network and the customer relationships (the durable asset); the hardware is the wedge that rides on top and refreshes every ~5 years.

Two phases — de-risked on purpose

  • Phase 1 — Managed brokerage (now): buy GPU capacity wholesale on the open market, wrap it in a managed service, resell it. Asset-light, immediate revenue, and it proves demand and signs the buyers (offtake) that de-risk Phase 2.
  • Phase 2 — Owned capacity (next): deploy owned GPU clusters on power-ready sites, financed against those contracts — infrastructure financed against signed revenue, not equity.

Why now

Three things line up: inference demand is inflecting; centralized supply is visibly stuck behind power and permitting; and big players are openly exploring distributed compute. Timing windows like this don't stay open.

Key takeaways
  • The constraint on AI is power + build-time, not just chips.
  • Go where power already is → deploy in days, not years.
  • The moat is distribution (sites + customers); hardware is a refreshing wedge.
  • Brokerage first proves demand and funds/secures the owned build.

Module 02

AI & GPUs 101 — what we're actually selling

Training vs. inference

Training builds a model — one huge, expensive compute job. Inference runs the finished model in production to answer requests, and it never stops. Every chatbot reply, image, or recommendation is an inference. As AI ships into products, inference is overtaking training as the dominant, steady, 24/7 load — which is exactly the demand a capacity business wants.

Why inference is a great business to serve

  • Steady, not bursty: predictable utilization (unlike training spikes).
  • Latency-sensitive: users want fast answers, so locality has value.
  • Growing with every AI feature shipped into real apps.

What a GPU is, and why

A GPU does enormous amounts of simple math in parallel. AI is mostly matrix multiplication at scale — exactly what GPUs excel at and CPUs are slow at. NVIDIA dominates (H100, H200, Blackwell B200). Two things matter most: raw compute and VRAM (on-chip memory) — bigger models need more memory. NVIDIA's software ecosystem (CUDA) also creates real lock-in.

The unit: a GPU-hour

Compute is bought and sold in GPU-hours — one GPU for one hour. You rent by the hour or reserve monthly. Heliode buys GPU-hours wholesale and resells them managed.

What "managed" actually includes

The value we add on top of raw capacity: provisioning (spinning up the right machines), orchestration (making many machines behave as one), monitoring & reliability, one endpoint to point at, and one bill. The customer ships their model; we handle the metal.

The supply ladder

  • Inference = running models live, 24/7 — steady, latency-sensitive demand.
  • GPUs do AI's parallel math; compute + VRAM are what matter; the unit is the GPU-hour.
  • "Managed" = provisioning, orchestration, reliability, one endpoint, one bill.
  • We arbitrage the price ladder: buy cheap/spot, sell managed under hyperscaler rates.

Module 03

The silicon landscape — who makes the compute

We resell and deploy chips, so you should know them cold: the types of processors, who designs them, who physically makes them, what the names mean, and who's fighting whom. This is the map under everything else in the course.

The types of processors

ChipWhat it isGood at
CPU
Central Processing Unit		A few powerful, flexible cores. The general-purpose "brain."Logic, sequential tasks, running the OS
GPU
Graphics Processing Unit		Thousands of small cores doing math in parallel. The AI workhorse — what we sell.Training & inference (parallel matrix math)
TPU / ASIC
Tensor Proc. Unit / custom chip		A chip hard-wired for one job. TPUs are Google's AI ASIC.Max efficiency on one workload
FPGA
Field-Programmable Gate Array		A chip you can re-wire after manufacture.Flexible, low-volume specialized work
NPU
Neural Processing Unit		A small AI accelerator built into phones/laptops.On-device AI (Apple Neural Engine, etc.)

For our business, GPUs are the product. The rest matter because they shape the competition — especially custom ASICs the cloud giants build to escape paying NVIDIA.

Who makes them — designers vs. manufacturers

The single most important distinction in chips: designing a chip and manufacturing one are two different businesses.

  • Fabless designers design chips but don't build them: NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom. NVIDIA designs the H100 — it doesn't make it.
  • Foundries physically manufacture chips for others: TSMC (Taiwan) makes the vast majority of the world's leading-edge chips, including NVIDIA's. Samsung (Korea) is #2.
  • IDMs do both — design and make: Intel is the classic example (now also opening its foundry to others).
  • Memory is its own industry: HBM (high-bandwidth memory) — the scarce memory stacked next to AI GPUs — is made by SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron. HBM supply is often the real bottleneck, not the GPU die.
  • The toolmaker: ASML (Netherlands) is the only company that makes the EUV lithography machines required to print the most advanced chips. No ASML, no leading-edge silicon.
Why this matters to you: when a customer asks "why are H100s so scarce and expensive?" the answer is this chain — one designer (NVIDIA), one dominant foundry (TSMC), one memory bottleneck (HBM), one toolmaker (ASML). Concentration at every step = scarcity, and scarcity is the spread we sell into.

Where they're made (and why it's fragile)

StepWhereRisk
Leading-edge logic (the GPU itself)Taiwan (TSMC) — ~90% of the most advancedGeopolitical (Taiwan/China)
Memory (HBM/DRAM)South Korea, USA (Micron)Tight supply, few players

The whole world's AI runs through a handful of facilities in a few countries. That's why governments are spending billions to reshore it — the U.S. CHIPS Act is funding TSMC in Arizona, Intel in Ohio/Arizona, and Samsung in Texas. The fragility is the opportunity: scarce supply that favors big buyers leaves the long tail underserved — our wedge.

What the names actually mean

NVIDIA names each architecture (generation) after a scientist, then numbers the chips within it:

NameDecode
H100 / H200H = Hopper (Grace Hopper). 100 = flagship data-center die; 200 = a refresh with more/faster memory.
A100A = Ampere (André-Marie Ampère) — the prior data-center generation.
L40SL = Ada Lovelace architecture; 40 = its tier; S = a higher-performance variant. Power-efficient, great for inference.
B200 / GB200B = Blackwell (David Blackwell), the current top generation. "GB" = a Grace CPU + Blackwell GPU paired.
Rubin (next)Named for astronomer Vera Rubin — NVIDIA's upcoming generation.

Suffixes describe the form factor, not the brain: SXM (high-power module for servers) vs PCIe (a card). And "4nm / 3nm" process names are now marketing for a generation of manufacturing — not a literal measurement. Others follow similar logic: AMD's Instinct MI300X (MI = Machine Intelligence), Google's TPU v5e/v5p (e = efficiency, p = performance), AWS Trainium (training) and Inferentia (inference).

The competition — who's fighting whom

"Competition" in AI compute happens at several layers. Know which one you're actually in.

  1. Chip designers: NVIDIA (dominant) vs AMD (Instinct) vs Intel (Gaudi) — and the hyperscalers building their own chips to stop paying NVIDIA: Google TPU, AWS Trainium/Inferentia, Microsoft Maia, Meta MTIA.
  2. The software moat: NVIDIA's real lock-in is CUDA, its software ecosystem. AMD's ROCm is trying to break it. Whoever owns the developer wins — which is why NVIDIA's lead is about more than the chip.
  3. Foundries: TSMC vs Samsung vs Intel Foundry — the race to manufacture the most advanced node.
  4. Memory: SK Hynix vs Samsung vs Micron for the HBM that every AI GPU needs.
  5. Compute providers — this is our layer: hyperscalers (AWS/GCP/Azure) vs neoclouds (CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe) vs marketplaces (Vast, RunPod) vs distributed players (Span + NVIDIA's XFRA).
Heliode's position: we are deliberately agnostic at the chip layer. We don't bet on who wins the chip war — we buy whatever delivers the best price-performance (H100/H200, L40S, or AMD if the economics win) and sell it managed. The only competition that directly matters to us is layer 5 — the compute providers — and we beat them on cheaper + managed + local + fast.
Key takeaways
  • Types: CPU (brain), GPU (our product), TPU/ASIC (custom), FPGA (re-wirable), NPU (on-device).
  • Designing ≠ making: NVIDIA designs, TSMC (Taiwan) makes, SK Hynix/Samsung/Micron supply HBM, ASML makes the one machine it all depends on.
  • Most advanced chips come from a few facilities in a few countries — that fragility is the scarcity we sell into.
  • Names = scientist (architecture) + number (tier): H = Hopper, A = Ampere, L = Lovelace, B = Blackwell.
  • We're chip-agnostic; our fight is at the compute-provider layer, not the chip war.

Module 04

The compute market & its economics

Demand drivers

Every AI feature shipped into a product adds permanent inference load. It's steady (24/7), latency-sensitive, and compounding as adoption grows — a utility-like demand curve, which is what you want underneath a capacity business.

Supply — and why it's stuck

Supply can't keep up because the binding constraint is power, not chips. Grid interconnection queues run years; permitting is slow; data-center capex is enormous. A single dense GPU node can draw on the order of ~10 kW — which is why existing heavy electrical service (commercial/industrial) is such a leverage point: the power is already there.

Pricing & the spread

The same GPU-hour costs wildly different amounts depending on where you buy it. That spread is the business.

The arbitrage, concretely: an H100-hour costs roughly ~$2 wholesale on marketplaces. Heliode resells it managed at ~$3 — still well under the $5–10 a hyperscaler charges. The buyer saves; Heliode keeps ~$1/GPU-hr plus the managed-service premium.

The risks inside the spread

  • Spot variability: the cheapest capacity can be reclaimed or fluctuate — managing that reliability is part of what you're paid for.
  • Reserved vs. usage: if you commit to wholesale capacity but bill customers by usage, idle time eats margin. Match commitments to demand.

The competition (and your angle)

You compete with hyperscalers (price), neoclouds (price + locality), and DIY (you remove the headache). Your edge isn't just "cheaper GPUs" — it's cheaper + managed + local + fast to deploy. Compute will commoditize; being the one who can put it where it's needed, fast, won't.

Why brokerage works now

  • Asset-light: no hardware to buy — you resell others' capacity.
  • Immediate revenue and near-zero CAC under a quote-first offer.
  • It proves demand and produces the signed offtake that makes owned hardware financeable.
Key takeaways
  • Demand is a 24/7 utility; supply is throttled by power and build-time.
  • The price ladder creates a real spread — but watch spot variability and idle commitments.
  • Your edge is cheaper + managed + local + fast, not price alone.

Module 05

Market sizing — TAM, SAM, SOM

Investors want to know how big this can get. Three nested numbers answer that — from the whole universe down to what you can realistically capture.

TermWhat it meansHeliode
TAM
Total Addressable Market		Total spend if you served 100% of the entire market.AI compute / GPU infrastructure — hundreds of $B today → $1T+ by 2030.
SAM
Serviceable Available Market		The slice your specific product/model can serve.GPU-as-a-service you can resell/own — ~$15–25B → $100B+.
SOM
Serviceable Obtainable Market		The realistic share you can win in the next few years.Inference teams in Austin/SF/Seattle/Boston you can sign.

Top-down vs. bottoms-up

Top-down takes a giant analyst number and shaves a percentage off ("if we get 1% of a $100B market…"). Investors distrust it because any number times 1% looks big. Bottoms-up builds from reality: count customers × what they spend × your win rate. It earns respect because every input is defensible.

Bottoms-up SOM, worked: ~500 inference-heavy teams across your 4 metros × ~$15K/mo avg GPU spend = $90M/yr of nearby spend (your SAM-in-region). Win 15% over three years → ~$13.5M/yr revenue you can credibly point to — far stronger than "1% of a trillion."

How to defend your numbers

  • Cite sources for the big figures (Grand View, Fortune Business Insights, analyst reports) and date them.
  • Show the bottoms-up math transparently — let them check it.
  • Be conservative on win rate; let the upside be obvious without overclaiming.

How to present it

Lead with the massive TAM (ambition), then land on a credible bottoms-up SOM (believability). The big number gets attention; the small number gets the check.

Key takeaways
  • TAM = whole market, SAM = your serviceable slice, SOM = what you can realistically win.
  • Back the SOM with a bottoms-up count, never a percentage of the TAM.
  • Pitch both: huge TAM for upside, grounded SOM for credibility.

Module 06

Unit economics — does one unit make money?

Three margin words (so they don't trip you up)

  • Gross margin: revenue minus the direct cost of delivering it (here, wholesale GPU cost).
  • Net contribution: revenue minus all direct running costs of that unit (power, bandwidth, host fee) — before company overhead.
  • Net profit: what's left after company overhead, taxes, and financing.

Phase 1 — brokerage margin

Per GPU-hour: pay ~$2 wholesale, sell ~$3 → ~$1 gross margin (≈33%). A dedicated H100 run continuously (~730 hrs/mo): ~$2,190 revenue − ~$1,460 cost = ~$730 gross margin per GPU per month.

First real month, 5–6 clients: ~8 GPUs each ≈ 48 GPUs → ~$105K revenue, ~$70K cost, ~$35K gross profit/month — with near-zero CAC under the quote-first offer (you only spin up paid capacity once they say yes).

Phase 2 — owned node economics

Metric (base case)MonthlyAnnual
Gross compute revenue~$9,800~$118K
Direct opex~$2,200~$26K
Net contribution~$7,600~$91K

Payback ~year 3, and roughly +$170K net per node over 5 years. Two caveats that decide everything: hardware cost (~$220K is a planning number you must ground-truth with real quotes), and none of it works without contracted offtake.

The levers (run the sensitivity)

  • Realized price per GPU-hr and utilization (hours actually sold) drive revenue.
  • Hardware cost and the annual price decline drive the long-run return.
  • The conservative case must still clear amortization — if it doesn't, don't deploy until offtake/price improves.
Key takeaways
  • Know the three margins: gross, net contribution, net profit.
  • Brokerage: ~$730 gross margin per GPU/month, profit from month one, ~zero CAC.
  • Owned node: ~$7.6K/mo net, ~yr-3 payback — only with signed offtake and a ground-truthed BOM.

Module 07

Fundraising 101 — raising $2M on a SAFE

The basics of equity

Your company is divided into shares. Selling shares gives investors ownership (a %) and gives you cash. The list of who owns what is the cap table. Every raise dilutes existing owners (their % shrinks) — fine if the pie grows faster than your slice shrinks.

What a SAFE is

A SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) is the standard early instrument. The investor gives money now; it converts to shares later, at your next priced round. Fast and cheap — no need to price shares today.

  • Valuation cap: the max company value at which their money converts — the number that really sets ownership. Lower cap = more ownership for them.
  • Discount: sometimes added — they convert at, say, 20% below the next round's price.
  • MFN: "most favored nation" — an early SAFE can adopt better terms you give later investors.
  • Post-money SAFE (the YC standard) makes dilution easy to calculate.

The math that matters

Ownership = investment ÷ post-money valuation.

RaiseCap (post-money)You give up
$2M$8M~25%
$2M$10M~20%
$2M$13M~15%

A healthy pre-seed gives up ~15–25%. Price too high (giving up only 5%) and investors balk — and you risk a painful down round next time. SAFEs also stack: several SAFEs can convert together at the priced round, so track total dilution.

Structure & who you raise from

  • Heliode, Inc. (Delaware C-corp) is the operating company investors expect — set it up (Stripe Atlas / Clerky / Carta). Issue founder stock to yourself (fully QSBS-eligible — up to $10M+ of gains potentially federal-tax-free after a 5-year hold), and file the 83(b) within 30 days.
  • Angels write $25–100K; pre-seed funds lead with $250–500K; accelerators (YC, Capital Factory) add capital + network.
  • A lead investor sets terms and anchors the round; others fill in behind them.
  • Your data room is the gated investor portal you already built — send it after a good first call.

How to run it

Batch it. Line up many conversations so they overlap — competition is what raises your cap and creates urgency. Use the teaser to get the meeting, the portal as the data room, the pipeline tracker to manage it. Two follow-ups max, then move on. Be ready to say clearly what the $2M buys (team, software, brokerage launch, validation sprint, a few proof nodes).

The one rule you don't break

Selling shares is a securities transaction. Keep it to accredited investors under Regulation D and use a startup lawyer. Don't freelance this part.

Key takeaways
  • A SAFE = money now, shares later; the valuation cap sets ownership.
  • $2M on a ~$10–13M cap ≈ 15–20% dilution — a normal, healthy pre-seed.
  • Delaware C-corp + 83(b) + lawyer + accredited investors. Lead first, then batch the fill.

Module 09

The energy model & the incentive stack — why we own the solar

Phase 2 puts owned GPU nodes on real sites, and the most important of those sites is a service-upgraded home. This module explains the priority deployment — the home, the panel upgrade, the solar, the battery — and then the part that makes it more than an energy bill: the tax incentives Heliode captures because Heliode owns the power assets. If you understand this module, you understand why the energy layer is a profit center, not a cost.

The priority deployment — what actually goes on the site

The flagship Phase-2 unit is a single-family home upgraded into a power-ready compute site. Concretely, it has four parts:

  • A service upgrade to 400A. Most homes have 100–200A electrical service. Our crew upgrades the main panel to 400A commercial-grade service so the site can carry a dense GPU node without tripping anything or starving the house.
  • A GPU node. The compute itself — an 8-GPU server that runs 24/7. Running continuously, a node of this density draws on the order of 240 kWh/day. Power is the node's single largest operating cost, so anything that offsets it goes straight to the bottom line.
  • A rooftop solar array of roughly 20+ kW. A ~20 kW array in a decent climate produces on the order of ~100 kWh/day.
  • A battery. Stores solar production, smooths the load, and provides a resilience layer if the grid blips.
Be honest about the math. The node needs ~240 kWh/day; the ~20 kW solar array makes ~100 kWh/day. That covers a meaningful chunk of the node's largest cost — but not all of it. This is not an off-grid system. Solar + battery is a cost-offset and a resilience layer that the grid backstops, not a claim that the node powers itself. Say it that way to investors and partners — overclaiming "off-grid AI" is how you lose credibility.

The incentive stack — and why Heliode gets it

Here is the part most people miss. There are two different solar tax credits in the U.S. tax code, and who owns the equipment decides which one applies:

  • The residential homeowner credit (former §25D) — the one a homeowner would have claimed on their own roof — expired at the end of 2025. A homeowner installing solar today gets essentially nothing from it.
  • The commercial Clean Electricity Investment Tax Credit (§48E) is claimed by a business that owns the energy asset. It is still very much alive.

This is the whole point: Heliode the company owns the solar array and the battery as a business asset — the homeowner does not. Because Heliode owns it, Heliode is the business that places the equipment in service, and Heliode claims the commercial §48E credit, a 30% base credit. Systems under 1 MW auto-qualify for the full rate (they skip the prevailing-wage/apprenticeship labor rules that larger projects must meet). Ownership is what unlocks the value — the residential path is dead, the commercial path is open, and we are on the commercial path by design.

On top of the 30% base, §48E has stackable adders:

  • +10% domestic content — for using a qualifying share of U.S.-made equipment.
  • +10% energy community — for siting in a qualifying area (e.g. former fossil-fuel or brownfield communities).

Stacked, the credit can reach up to ~50% of the eligible cost of the solar + battery system.

The second shield — 100% bonus depreciation

The tax credit is not the only benefit. Because the solar + battery is a business asset, Heliode also depreciates it — writes its cost off against taxable income. The 2025 tax law restored 100% first-year bonus depreciation for assets placed in service after January 2025. The equipment uses a 5-year MACRS schedule, but bonus depreciation lets Heliode deduct the full depreciable basis in year one. That is a second tax shield stacked on top of the §48E credit — the credit reduces tax owed directly, and the depreciation reduces the taxable income underneath it.

The battery is the durable piece — and a revenue stream

Solar and storage are treated slightly differently under the rules, and the difference favors batteries:

  • Battery storage retains the full §48E through ~2033 — beyond the tighter deadlines tightening around solar. So the storage asset keeps its credit longer.
  • A battery can export to the grid and participate in VPP (virtual power plant) and demand-response programs — where a utility pays you to discharge stored energy (or curtail load) at moments of peak grid stress. That makes the battery a secondary revenue stream, not just backup.

The timing lesson — solar has a deadline, the battery has runway

Two different clocks. For solar, the full 30% credit favors projects that begin construction by mid-2026, and tightens after that — so the solar credit is best thought of as optimizable upside. For battery storage, the full §48E runs through ~2033. That distinction matters for us: our owned-node rollout is realistically a 2027-and-beyond story, so we don't bank the model on racing the solar deadline. We anchor on the battery credit's long runway, capture whatever solar credit is available when each site is built, and treat anything extra as upside rather than a calendar we're chasing.

Why this matters to the business: the energy assets aren't a cost we tolerate to power the node — they're a profit center we own. The §48E credit (up to ~50%), 100% bonus depreciation, and VPP/demand-response revenue together can offset a large share of the system's cost, while the solar offsets the node's biggest operating expense. Owning the power is what turns a residential site into an asset with three stacked returns: compute margin, energy-cost offset, and tax/grid value.

Put real numbers on it — one site, back-of-envelope

Let's run a single site so the stack is concrete. Say the ~20+ kW solar + battery system costs $80,000 installed, and Heliode pays the 21% federal corporate tax rate. Take the conservative 30% base case (no adders yet):

  • §48E credit @ 30% of $80,000 = $24,000. This is a credit — it comes straight off the tax bill dollar-for-dollar, not just off income.
  • Depreciation. When you take a credit, the IRS makes you reduce the asset's depreciable basis by half the credit. So basis = $80,000 − $12,000 = $68,000. With 100% bonus depreciation you deduct that whole $68,000 in year one; at a 21% rate that deduction is worth ~$14,300 in saved tax.
  • Add them up: $24,000 + $14,300 = ≈ $38,000 of first-year tax benefit on an $80,000 system — so the net first-year cost is ≈ $42,000, roughly half.

Now the upside case. If the site earns both +10% adders (domestic content + energy community), the credit is 50% = $40,000, the basis drops to $60,000 (a ~$12,600 depreciation shield), and the first-year benefit climbs to ~$53,000 — netting the system down to ~$27,000, about a third of sticker. And none of that counts the grid power the solar offsets every month, or the VPP / demand-response revenue the battery can earn on top.

The lesson in one line: owning an ~$80k energy system, you can get roughly $38k–$53k of it back in year one through the credit + depreciation — so the asset that powers your node, adds resilience, and can sell power back to the grid effectively costs you half to a third of its sticker price. (One caveat: bonus depreciation only helps if you have taxable income — or a carryforward — to absorb it.)

We take your installed base, your crews, and your power expertise and turn them into the one thing the entire AI industry is starving for: deployed, powered compute. You stop being a company that offsets a power bill and become the energy-and-deployment layer of an AI compute network."

Span and NVIDIA just validated this with XFRA — and XFRA's stated next move is the retrofit solar base, which is Sunrun's turf. So the subtext is urgency: this is happening to your base with or without you; partner with me and you lead it instead of getting disintermediated.

The ask

Don't ask for a treaty — ask for a pilot. "Pick one or two markets where you're strong and demand is real. Let's co-deploy a handful of nodes, prove the unit economics, and scale state-by-state on demand from there." Small, concrete, fast — and it makes the next conversation inevitable.

The endgame (hold this close)

There's a longer game on the board: if the partnership becomes integral enough — if Heliode is the compute brain riding on Sunrun's energy body — the natural path is that Sunrun acquires Heliode and you run the compute division. That's a real, attractive outcome. But it's a destination, not an opening line. You don't pitch it — you earn it by becoming indispensable and impossible to cleanly replicate. Two rules protect that path: (1) keep your optionality and your customer relationships yours, so you negotiate from strength, not need; (2) the more you own demand + deployment, the higher the price of buying you. Build like you'll stay independent; that's exactly what makes the acquisition offer good.

Glossary

GPU
The chip that runs AI's parallel math. Our product is access to GPUs.
VRAM
A GPU's on-chip memory; bigger AI models need more of it.
CUDA
NVIDIA's software ecosystem — a big reason NVIDIA dominates (lock-in). AMD's equivalent is ROCm.
CPU / TPU / ASIC / NPU
Other processor types: CPU = general-purpose brain; TPU/ASIC = chip hard-wired for one job (e.g. Google TPU); NPU = small on-device AI accelerator.
HBM
High-Bandwidth Memory — the fast memory stacked next to an AI GPU. Made by SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron; often the real supply bottleneck.
Fabless / Foundry / IDM
Fabless = designs chips, doesn't make them (NVIDIA, AMD); foundry = manufactures for others (TSMC, Samsung); IDM = does both (Intel).
TSMC
Taiwan Semiconductor — the foundry that manufactures ~90% of the world's most advanced chips, including NVIDIA's.
ASML
The Dutch company that makes the EUV lithography machines required to print leading-edge chips — a single point of failure for the whole industry.
GPU-hour
One GPU running for one hour — the unit we buy wholesale and resell.
Inference
Running a trained AI model in production. The steady, 24/7 load we target.
Training
Building the model — a huge, one-time compute job (vs. continuous inference).
Node
One deployed unit of our hardware — an 8-GPU server (~$250K, ~10kW) on a power-ready site, with battery for backup.
Hyperscaler / Neocloud / Marketplace
The price ladder of GPU supply — expensive/convenient (AWS) to cheap/spot (Vast.ai).
Spot vs. reserved
Spot = cheap but reclaimable/variable; reserved = committed capacity you pay for whether used or not.
MRR
Monthly Recurring Revenue — predictable monthly income.
Gross margin / Net contribution / Net profit
Revenue minus direct delivery cost / minus all unit running costs / minus company overhead & taxes.
Opex / Capex
Ongoing operating expense vs. one-time capital expense (hardware).
Amortization
Spreading a one-time hardware cost across its useful life to see true monthly profit.
Utilization
The share of hours a node is actually sold/working.
Offtake
A signed commitment from a buyer to purchase the compute.
LOI
Letter of Intent — a buyer's written (usually non-binding) intent to purchase.
CAC
Customer Acquisition Cost — what it costs to win a customer.
BOM
Bill of Materials — the itemized, quoted cost of everything in a node.
TAM / SAM / SOM
Total / Serviceable-available / Serviceable-obtainable market — the nested sizing numbers.
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity — money now that converts to shares later.
Valuation cap
The max company value at which a SAFE converts; it sets the investor's ownership.
Pre- / Post-money
Company value before / after the new investment is added.
Dilution
The reduction in existing owners' percentage when new shares are issued.
Lead investor
The investor who sets terms and anchors a round; others fill in behind them.
Data room
The package of materials investors review in diligence — here, your gated portal.
Runway / Burn
How long your cash lasts / how fast you spend it per month.
C-corp
The Delaware corporation structure investors expect for equity rounds.
83(b) election
A tax filing (within 30 days of founder stock) that can save you a lot later.
QSBS (§1202)
Qualified Small Business Stock — C-corp shares held 5+ years can exempt up to $10M+ of gains from federal tax. Holding the stock as an individual (or via a pass-through LLC) qualifies; a C-corp holding it would kill the benefit.
Reg D / Accredited investor
The private-placement rules; accredited investors meet income/net-worth thresholds.
Discovery
The part of a sales call where you ask and listen to learn the prospect's stack, spend, pain, decision-maker, and timeline.
Objection
A prospect's concern (price, reliability, switching). A buying signal to answer calmly — not a rejection.
Pilot
A short, paid trial on one workload that lets a prospect feel the service before committing fully.
Pipeline
All your active deals and their stages — tracked in the Kanban board so nothing warm goes cold.
Decision-maker
The person who can actually sign — usually a founder, CTO, or head of ML. Find them early.
