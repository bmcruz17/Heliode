Module 01

The big idea — reasoning from first principles

Start with the one sentence: Heliode sells AI computing power, and is building the distributed network that produces it — close to where the demand already is.

The bottleneck

Modern AI runs on GPUs. Demand for them is exploding. But you can't just conjure more: a GPU needs a building, and a building needs power — and the power grid is the slow part. New grid hookups (interconnection) can take 5–7 years, and a new data center is a multi-year, multi-billion-dollar build. So the true constraint on AI isn't only chips; it's power and time-to-build.

The first-principles move

If power and build-time are the constraint, don't fight the grid — go where power already exists. Commercial and light-industrial buildings already carry heavy electrical service. Put GPU capacity there, next to the teams that need it, and you stand up compute in days, not years.

Where the moat is

The hard part isn't the chips — anyone can buy those. The hard part is distribution: finding the sites, signing them, installing, and operating many locations as if they were one. That's exactly the part big players don't want to do, which is why it's defensible. Heliode owns the site network and the customer relationships; the hardware rides on top and refreshes every ~5 years.

Two phases (de-risked on purpose)

  • Phase 1 — Managed brokerage (now): buy GPU capacity wholesale on the open market, wrap it in a managed service, resell it. Asset-light, revenue immediately, and it proves demand and signs the buyers (offtake) that de-risk Phase 2.
  • Phase 2 — Owned capacity (next): deploy owned GPU clusters on power-ready sites, financed against those contracts — the way infrastructure is normally financed: against signed revenue, not equity.
Key takeaways
  • The constraint on AI is power + build-time, not just chips.
  • Go where power already is → deploy in days, not years.
  • The moat is distribution (sites + customers), not the hardware.
  • Brokerage first proves demand and funds/secures the owned build.
Module 02

AI & GPUs 101 — what we're actually selling

Training vs. inference

An AI model has two phases of compute. Training is building the model — a huge, one-time, expensive compute job. Inference is running the finished model in production to answer requests — and it never stops. Every chatbot reply, image, or recommendation is an inference. As AI products ship, inference is overtaking training as the dominant, steady, 24/7 compute load. That steady demand is exactly what Heliode targets.

What a GPU is, and why

A GPU (graphics processing unit) is a chip built to do enormous amounts of simple math in parallel. AI is mostly matrix multiplication at massive scale, which is precisely what GPUs are good at and CPUs are slow at. NVIDIA dominates (H100, H200, and the newer Blackwell generation). GPUs are scarce, expensive, and power-hungry — which is the whole game.

The unit: a GPU-hour

Compute is bought and sold in GPU-hours — one GPU running for one hour. You rent capacity by the hour (or reserve it monthly). Heliode buys GPU-hours wholesale and resells them as a managed service.

Who the players are (the supply ladder)

Key takeaways
  • Inference = running models live, 24/7 — our target load.
  • GPUs do AI's parallel math; the unit is the GPU-hour.
  • There's a price ladder from marketplaces (cheap) to hyperscalers (expensive); we arbitrage it.
Module 03

The compute market & its economics

Demand

Inference demand is steady, latency-sensitive, and growing fast as AI gets embedded in real products. Unlike training (bursty), inference is a 24/7 utility — ideal for a capacity business.

Supply (and why it's stuck)

Supply can't keep up because the binding constraint is power, not chips. Grid interconnection queues run years; permitting is slow; data-center capex is enormous. This is why distributing onto existing power is such a leverage point.

Pricing & the spread

The same GPU-hour costs wildly different amounts depending on where you buy it. That spread is the business.

The arbitrage, concretely: an H100-hour costs roughly ~$2 wholesale on marketplaces. Heliode resells it managed at ~$3 — still well under the $5–10 a hyperscaler charges. The buyer saves; Heliode keeps ~$1/GPU-hr plus the managed-service premium.

Why brokerage works now

  • Asset-light: no hardware to buy — you resell others' capacity.
  • Immediate revenue: margin shows up from month one.
  • It proves demand and produces the signed offtake that makes owned hardware financeable.
Key takeaways
  • Demand is a 24/7 utility; supply is throttled by power and build-time.
  • The price ladder creates a real spread you can capture.
  • Brokerage is the low-risk on-ramp that funds and de-risks the rest.
Module 04

Market sizing — TAM, SAM, SOM

Investors want to know how big this can get. Three nested numbers answer that — from the whole universe down to what you can realistically capture.

TermWhat it meansHeliode
TAM
Total Addressable Market		Total spend if you served 100% of the entire market.AI compute / GPU infrastructure — hundreds of $B today → $1T+ by 2030.
SAM
Serviceable Available Market		The slice your specific product/model can actually serve.GPU-as-a-service you can resell/own — ~$15–25B → $100B+.
SOM
Serviceable Obtainable Market		The realistic share you can win in the next few years.Inference teams in Austin/SF/Seattle/Boston you can sign.

Top-down vs. bottoms-up

Top-down takes a giant analyst number and shaves a percentage off ("if we get 1% of a $100B market…"). Investors distrust it. Bottoms-up builds from reality: count customers × what they spend × your win rate. That earns respect.

Bottoms-up SOM example: say there are ~500 inference-heavy teams across your 4 metros, spending an average ~$15K/mo on GPUs. That's $90M/yr of nearby spend. Win 15% over three years → ~$13.5M/yr in revenue you can credibly point to — far more convincing than "1% of a trillion."

How to present it

Lead with the massive TAM (ambition), then land on a credible bottoms-up SOM (believability). The big number gets attention; the small number gets the check.

Key takeaways
  • TAM = whole market, SAM = your serviceable slice, SOM = what you can realistically win.
  • Always back the SOM with a bottoms-up count, not a percentage of the TAM.
  • Pitch both: huge TAM for the upside, grounded SOM for credibility.
Module 05

Unit economics — does one unit make money?

Phase 1 — brokerage margin

Per GPU-hour you pay ~$2 wholesale and sell ~$3 → ~$1 gross margin (≈33%). A dedicated H100 run continuously (~730 hrs/mo) is ~$2,190 revenue − ~$1,460 cost = ~$730 gross margin per GPU per month.

First real month, 5–6 clients: at ~8 GPUs each that's ~48 GPUs → ~$105K revenue, ~$70K cost, ~$35K gross profit/month — with near-zero CAC under the quote-first offer (you only spin up paid capacity once they say yes).

Phase 2 — owned node

A single owned node (≈16 GPUs + battery) base case: ~$9,800/mo gross, ~$2,200/mo opex, ~$7,600/mo net contribution, ~year-3 payback, and roughly +$170K net per node over 5 years. The big caveat: hardware cost (~$220K is a planning number you must ground-truth with real quotes), and none of it works without contracted offtake.

The levers

  • Realized price per GPU-hr and utilization (hours actually sold) drive revenue.
  • Hardware cost and the annual price decline drive the long-run return.
  • Run the sensitivity grid before you commit capital — the conservative case must still clear amortization.
Key takeaways
  • Brokerage: ~$730 gross margin per GPU/month; profit from month one.
  • Owned node: ~$7.6K/mo net, ~yr-3 payback — but only with signed offtake.
  • Ground-truth the hardware BOM; price, utilization, and cost are the swing factors.
Module 06

Fundraising 101 — raising $2M on a SAFE

The basics of equity

Your company is divided into shares. Selling shares to investors gives them ownership (a %) and gives you cash. The full list of who owns what is the cap table. Every time you raise, existing owners are diluted (their % shrinks) — that's normal and fine if the pie grows.

What a SAFE is

A SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) is the standard early-stage instrument. The investor gives you money now; it converts into shares later (at your next priced round). It's fast and cheap — no need to set an exact share price today.

  • Valuation cap: the maximum company value at which their money converts — the number that really sets their ownership. Lower cap = more ownership for them.
  • Discount: sometimes added — they convert at, say, 20% below the next round's price.
  • Post-money SAFE (the YC standard) makes your dilution easy to calculate.

The math that matters

Ownership = investment ÷ post-money valuation. Raising $2M on a ~$10M cap ≈ 20% dilution. A healthy pre-seed gives up ~15–25%. Price too high (giving up only 5%) and investors balk and you risk a painful down round later.

Structure & who you raise from

  • Delaware C-corp is what investors expect — set it up (Clerky / Stripe Atlas / Carta), issue founder stock, and file your 83(b) within 30 days.
  • Angels write $25–100K; pre-seed funds lead with $250–500K; accelerators (YC, Capital Factory) add capital + network.
  • Run it as a batch: line up many conversations so they overlap — competition is what raises your cap and creates urgency.

The one rule you don't break

Selling shares is a securities transaction. Keep it to accredited investors under Regulation D and use a startup lawyer. Don't freelance this part.

Key takeaways
  • A SAFE = money now, shares later; the valuation cap sets ownership.
  • $2M on a ~$10M cap ≈ 20% dilution — a normal, healthy pre-seed.
  • Delaware C-corp + 83(b) + a lawyer + accredited investors. Batch the raise.
Module 07

Risks & the 90-day validation sprint

Honest founders name the risks and attack them. The biggest ones, in order:

  1. Site/host acquisition — the hardest problem. De-risk by starting on power-ready commercial/light-industrial sites, not cold residential.
  2. Hardware cost — the ~$220K node is an estimate; get vendor quotes before committing capital.
  3. Offtake — no buyer, no business. The brokerage exists to produce signed demand.
  4. Financing — hardware is financed against contracts; that needs real offtake first.
  5. Regulatory/permitting, price decline, GPU supply, and team scope — manage early, plan conservatively.

The sprint (next 90 days)

  • A vendor-quoted hardware BOM.
  • 3–5 power-ready test sites with measured power, noise, install complexity.
  • 2–3 buyer contracts or LOIs via the brokerage.
  • An Austin electrical & permitting read.
  • The model refreshed with real numbers.

Clearing these turns the pilot from a concept into an underwritable plan — and lets you raise at a higher number, honestly.

Key takeaways
  • Name the risks; site acquisition and hardware cost are the big two.
  • Prove the hard parts in 90 days before the pilot raise.
  • Traction — real BOM, sites, and LOIs — is what earns a higher valuation.

Glossary

GPU
The chip that runs AI's parallel math. Our product is access to GPUs.
GPU-hour
One GPU running for one hour — the unit we buy wholesale and resell.
Inference
Running a trained AI model in production. The steady, 24/7 load we target.
Training
Building the model — a huge, one-time compute job (vs. inference, which is continuous).
Node
One deployed unit of our hardware (a server with ~16 GPUs + battery) on a host site.
Hyperscaler / Neocloud / Marketplace
The price ladder of GPU supply — from expensive/convenient (AWS) to cheap/spot (Vast.ai).
MRR
Monthly Recurring Revenue — predictable monthly income.
Net contribution
Revenue minus a node's direct running costs, before company overhead and taxes.
Opex / Capex
Ongoing operating expense vs. one-time capital expense (hardware).
Amortization
Spreading a one-time hardware cost across its useful life to see true monthly profit.
Utilization
The share of hours a node is actually sold/working.
Offtake
A signed commitment from a buyer to purchase the compute.
LOI
Letter of Intent — a buyer's written (usually non-binding) intent to purchase.
CAC
Customer Acquisition Cost — what it costs to win a customer.
BOM
Bill of Materials — the itemized, quoted cost of everything in a node.
TAM / SAM / SOM
Total / Serviceable-available / Serviceable-obtainable market — the nested sizing numbers.
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity — money now that converts to shares later.
Valuation cap
The max company value at which a SAFE converts; it sets the investor's ownership.
Pre- / Post-money
Company value before / after the new investment is added.
Dilution
The reduction in existing owners' percentage when new shares are issued.
C-corp
The Delaware corporation structure investors expect for equity rounds.
83(b) election
A tax filing (within 30 days of getting founder stock) that can save you a lot later.
Reg D / Accredited investor
The private-placement rules; accredited investors meet income/net-worth thresholds.
