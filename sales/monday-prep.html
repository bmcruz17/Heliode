1 · Who I'm selling to (the ICP)

A company already spending heavily on AWS / GCP / Azure to run an AI model in production — technical enough to know their setup, but who hasn't realized there's a cheaper way.

If they're not running a model in production yet, or have no real monthly cloud bill, they're not my customer. Don't burn time on them — follow up next quarter.

The qualifying signal is the monthly GPU bill:

  • Under ~$5k/mo — small fish. Take them, but don't chase.
  • $5k–$50k/mo — my bread and butter. Build the base here.
  • $50k+/mo — whale. Great, but one whale walking can't sink the bandwidth commitment.
Portfolio rule (from Zach): aim for a majority of mid-size accounts, a few whales, and some small fish. A single big customer leaving shouldn't jeopardize my committed spend with RunPod.

2 · Words to know (so I never freeze)

Inference — running a trained model to get answers (what my customers do). Not training, which is building the model.
H100 / H200 — flagship NVIDIA GPUs, high demand. These are what I can actually serve well and sell.
A100 / L40S — older/cheaper GPUs. Zach said adding these "won't add much" — lower demand. Don't lead with them.
3090 / 4090 — consumer GPUs (24GB each). Can stitch ~4 together → ~96GB for smaller, sharded workloads. Cheap, niche.
VRAM — the GPU's memory. Bigger models need more. A 70B model needs far more than a 7B model.
On-demand — pay per hour, no money up front, no commitment. This is how I start customers — removes their risk.
Committed / reserved spend — promise a baseline of usage for a discount. RunPod: ~10% low end → ~30% at ~$1M/yr.
Serverless — capacity that scales to zero when idle; you pay only when it runs.
Margin over spot — my profit = what I charge the customer minus what I pay RunPod.
Ramp — a starting baseline that steps up as I hit performance targets over 2–3 months. Can go up, not down.
Docker / container — the package the model runs in. RunPod runs on Docker; I deploy containers onto their capacity.

3 · Discovery call script

My job on a discovery call is not to pitch — it's to ask 5 questions and listen. Keep this in front of me.

Open (say it the same way every time)

"Thanks for hopping on. I'll keep this short. I'm not going to pitch you — I just want to understand what you're running and what you're paying, and if there's a cheaper way I'll show you. Cool if I ask a few quick questions?"

The 5 questions

1. What model do you have in production right now? (listen for size — 7B vs 70B changes everything)
2. What are you running it on today? (AWS? which instances — p4d, p5, g5? tells me their GPU + sophistication)
3. Roughly what's the monthly GPU / compute bill? (this is what qualifies them — see the tiers in §1)
4. Which GPUs, and how many? (H100/H200 = I can help; A100/L40S = limited)
5. What's the biggest pain — cost, availability, or the ops headache? (this is what I sell against)

Close (always end with a next step — never "I'll send info")

"This is really helpful. Based on what you're describing, I think I can get you [rough %] cheaper with no money up front and no infra change. Let me put together exact numbers — can I send those over and grab 20 minutes [day] to walk through them?"
